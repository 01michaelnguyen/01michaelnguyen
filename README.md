# About me

Hi there! I am currently an Applied Mathematics PhD student studying at UCL, London, supervised by Edward Johnson. I build, simple, theoretical models from first principles that describe waves around the Arctic Ocean, and also I model how rivers leaves coastal outflows and the subsequent waves it generates there. The purpose of my PhD is to explain if I can create a theoretical model, that only incorporates topographic effects and wind-forcing, that qualitatively describes how waves and wind-stress are distributed around the ocean. 

I am currently working on creating a two-basin (and more realistic geometries in this framework) model of the Arctic Ocean: this Ocean is highly steep, varying greatly in topography compared to the other oceans, hence circulation around these polar regions are a little different than standard, mid-latitude oceans (eg. the Atlantic Ocean). Right now, I am seeing how waves freely move around the ocean without the effects of winds; these can be solved analytically via various mathematical methods, which are the free modes of the system. I am applying this method to a variety of geometries that extends and makes the idealised two-basin setup more realistic (such as changing the individual sizes of these basins; another basin to incorporate smaller seas etc.). 

Closer towards the end of my studies, I aim to add wind-forcing effects, which can also be solved analytically. I then aim to solve these models on the nonlinear equations  on various PDE solvers in Python. Currently, I have numerically simulated small-scale models using the Dedalus package, which is able to solve these equations on these complex (conformal grid) geometries.

I am looking forward to simulate large-scale models of the Arctic Ocean using the full equations (Shallow-Water or rotating Navier-Stokes equations), which involves using the fast Oceananigans package in Julia, in conjunction with Matthew Crowe.
